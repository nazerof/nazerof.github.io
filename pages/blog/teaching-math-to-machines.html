From Teaching Math to Teaching Machines

Nazer Hdaifeh March 2026 Career & Perspective 8 min read

The first time I saw a confusion matrix, I laughed. Not because it was confusing — but because it was familiar. I had been building them by hand for years. I just called them “assessment rubrics.”

Rows for what the student actually understood. Columns for what my test said they understood. The off-diagonal cells — those were the interesting ones. The student who aced the exam but couldn't explain the concept to a classmate (false positive). The quiet kid in the back row who failed the test but asked the one question that proved she understood the deeper structure (false negative). I spent years staring at those mismatches, trying to build better instruments to tell the difference. I just didn't know the vocabulary yet.

I taught mathematics for eight years in Riyadh, Saudi Arabia — first as a classroom teacher at Advanced Schools, eventually as a department manager directing an American diploma program. In 2018, I left for Denmark to study Mathematical Modeling at DTU. By 2022, I was a data scientist. People describe this as a career change. I've come to see it as something closer to a translation — the same core work, expressed in a different language.

What follows is not career advice. It's an attempt to articulate something I didn't fully understand until I was deep into my second career: the structural parallels between teaching and machine learning are not metaphorical. They are architectural.

Curriculum Design Is Architecture

When you design a semester of mathematics, you don't just pick topics and arrange them chronologically. You build a dependency graph. Fractions before ratios. Ratios before proportional reasoning. Proportional reasoning before linear functions. Linear functions before systems of equations. If you get the sequencing wrong, nothing downstream works. Students don't just struggle — they develop misconceptions that compound, and by March you're fighting errors that were seeded in September.

The first time I designed a data pipeline, I felt an uncanny recognition. The logic was identical. Data ingestion before cleaning. Cleaning before feature engineering. Feature engineering before model training. The dependency structure of a well-designed pipeline is a curriculum. And the failure modes are the same: garbage in one stage doesn't just degrade the next stage — it poisons everything downstream in ways that are maddeningly difficult to diagnose.

There's a concept in education called “spiral curriculum,” introduced by Jerome Bruner. You revisit core concepts at increasing levels of sophistication. You teach fractions in grade five, revisit them as rational expressions in grade nine, and again as elements of quotient fields if the student studies abstract algebra. Each pass deepens the representation without discarding the previous one.

This is iterative model refinement. Start with a baseline. Revisit with more features, more data, more sophisticated architectures. Each iteration deepens the model's representation without discarding the learning from the previous one. The lesson plan is the DAG. I just didn't have the acronym at the time.

Feature Importance and Pedagogical Intuition

A veteran teacher develops something that's difficult to articulate but impossible to fake: an intuition for which concepts carry the most explanatory weight. After a few years in the classroom, you learn that if a student truly understands the concept of a function — not the notation, not the procedure, but the idea that an input maps to exactly one output — then calculus, linear algebra, and probability will eventually click. Miss that concept, and the student can memorize derivatives all day without understanding what they mean.

That's feature importance. Not in the SHAP-value, computed-from-a-trained-model sense, but in the deeper sense: knowing which variables in a system carry real explanatory power and which are noise. Years of watching students struggle taught me to identify the load-bearing ideas — the ones where understanding cascades forward and misunderstanding cascades into confusion.

When I sit down with a new dataset, I find myself applying the same instinct. Not just running correlation matrices, but asking: which of these variables, if you truly understood them, would make the rest of the data comprehensible? Which features are the “concept of a function” for this problem — the ones where getting it right unlocks everything downstream?

This isn't something I learned from a textbook. It was trained into me by eight years of standing in front of thirty teenagers, watching their faces for the moment something either clicked or collapsed.

Evaluation Is Everything

In education, we distinguish between formative and summative assessment. Formative assessment is the quick check during a lesson — a question, a mini-quiz, a thumbs-up-thumbs-down — that tells you whether students are tracking before you move on. Summative assessment is the final exam: the comprehensive evaluation after learning is supposed to be complete.

In machine learning, we call these validation and test sets. And the core lesson is the same: if you only evaluate at the end, you've already failed. The entire point of formative assessment is to catch problems while you can still fix them. The entire point of a validation set is to catch overfitting before you commit to your model.

But the deeper parallel is in the failure modes of evaluation itself. Every experienced teacher knows that a well-designed test can still produce misleading results. The student who memorizes the textbook examples and reproduces them perfectly on the exam — that's overfitting. They've learned the training data, not the underlying distribution. Put them in a slightly different context, change the numbers, rephrase the question, and they collapse. The student who “gets it” will handle the novel problem because they learned the structure, not the surface.

This is exactly the train/test split problem. And the solution in both domains is the same: evaluate on examples the learner hasn't seen before, in contexts that require transfer rather than memorization. In education, we call these “application questions.” In ML, we call it cross-validation. The principle is identical: generalization is the only metric that matters.

A student who scores 95% on problems identical to the homework but freezes on a novel application question has overfit. A model with 99% training accuracy and 60% test accuracy has overfit. The diagnosis is the same. So is the remedy: more diverse training data, less reliance on pattern-matching, and evaluation that tests transfer.

I spent years designing assessments that could distinguish between genuine understanding and sophisticated memorization. When I started designing model evaluation frameworks, I realized I'd been doing cross-validation by hand — just with pencils instead of Python.

The Patience of Iteration

Teaching twenty-five teenagers calculus is an exercise in controlled frustration. You explain the chain rule. You work through examples. You assign practice. The next day, half the class has forgotten how to differentiate a polynomial, let alone a composite function. So you try again. You change the explanation. You draw different diagrams. You find a new analogy. You iterate.

And the hard part isn't the iteration itself. The hard part is maintaining the discipline to distinguish between two fundamentally different situations: the approach is working and just needs more iterations, versus the approach is fundamentally wrong and more iterations will only deepen the failure. A good teacher knows when to be patient and when to throw out the lesson plan entirely. That judgment — knowing whether you're converging slowly or diverging — is the core skill.

Hyperparameter tuning requires exactly this judgment. You're watching a loss curve and asking: is this model still learning, or has it plateaued? Should I give it more epochs, or is the architecture wrong? Should I adjust the learning rate, or do I need to rethink the features? The patience to let a promising approach converge, combined with the honesty to abandon a failing one — that's the same muscle in both domains.

Teaching also gave me something that I think is undervalued in engineering: comfort with partial progress. A student who moves from “completely lost” to “understands the concept but makes arithmetic errors” has made enormous progress, even if their test score only moved from 30 to 55. In ML, a model that moves from random guessing to directionally correct but noisy predictions has made the critical leap. The ability to see that progress — to not throw out the approach just because the numbers aren't yet where you want them — is something the classroom teaches through sheer repetition.

A Translation, Not a Pivot

When people hear my background, they sometimes express surprise that a math teacher could become a data scientist. The implication is that these are distant fields requiring fundamentally different skills. I've come to believe the opposite: they're the same field, applied to different substrates.

Teaching is the practice of making complex systems understandable and reliable. You take a body of abstract knowledge, structure it so that a learner can build understanding incrementally, design evaluation systems to verify that understanding is genuine, and iterate relentlessly when it isn't. That sentence also describes machine learning engineering.

The substrates are different. In one case, the learner is a teenager in Riyadh trying to understand why x can represent any number. In the other, the learner is a neural network in a data center trying to understand which patterns in the input predict the output. But the work — the sequencing, the evaluation, the iteration, the patience, the intuition for what carries signal — is the same work.

I didn't change careers. I changed classrooms. The students are different. The curriculum is written in Python now. But when I'm debugging a model at midnight, trying to figure out why it's learning the wrong thing, I'm doing exactly what I did in Riyadh at midnight: staring at the evidence, asking where the understanding broke down, and redesigning the lesson.
