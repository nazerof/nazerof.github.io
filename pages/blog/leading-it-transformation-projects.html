Shipping the Innobooster Project: Notes on Leading Technical Transformation

April 2026 Project Management 9 min read

In late 2023, the company I worked for at the time received an Innobooster grant from the Danish Innovation Fund to unify our fragmented data pipelines into a single modern architecture, add AI-driven enrichment, and prove the approach could scale. I was the technical lead and the project manager for that work. Grant-funded projects are peculiar beasts: you have a fixed budget, a fixed scope on paper, a fixed timeline, and a panel of people who are going to ask pointed questions about all three at specific checkpoints. This is what I learned about leading that kind of work.

The core lesson is that the parts of the project that look like project management and the parts that look like engineering are not separable. When I tried to separate them, both suffered. When I treated them as the same job with different surfaces, the project moved.

The Plan Nobody Reads Is the Plan That Matters

Every grant-funded project starts with a Gantt chart. Ours had milestones, dependencies, deliverables, and the appropriate number of coloured bars. Nobody ever looked at it again after the kickoff meeting. That was fine, because the real plan was not the chart. The real plan was the shared understanding of what we were trying to do, held in the heads of maybe six people, and reinforced by a one-page status note I sent out every two weeks.

What the chart was good for was the steering group. What the one-pager was good for was getting the work done. A project manager who thinks these are the same artefact is going to struggle. The steering group needs to see progress in a format that matches their mental model of what progress looks like: coloured bars, budget consumed vs. remaining, risks flagged, decisions needed. The team needs to see progress in a format that matches their actual work: what is in flight, what is blocked, what was decided, what changed since last time. These are different documents. Pretending otherwise makes both worse.

The other thing I learned about plans: write down decisions. Not meeting minutes, not Slack threads, but a short, dated list of decisions in a single document. "On March 4, we decided to use Azure Data Factory rather than Databricks for the ingestion layer, primarily because of existing team familiarity." Three months later, when someone asks why a particular choice was made, you can answer without reconstruction. Six months later, when circumstances have changed and the decision should be revisited, you have a starting point. Decisions decay fast. Written decisions decay slower.

Stakeholders Are Not a Monolith

When I started the project, I had a mental model with one bucket called "stakeholders." Over the course of the work, that bucket had to be split into at least four.

The steering group cared about budget, timeline, scope, and risk. They did not want to hear about the specific challenges of OCR bounding boxes or why we were switching Python libraries. They wanted to know whether we were on track, whether we needed help, and whether any of the things they were counting on for next quarter were now at risk.

The funding body cared about a specific question: is this project advancing the things we gave you money to advance? They wanted evidence of innovation, measurable outcomes, and a credible story about why this work was worth funding. Their reporting requirements were structured and formal. You cannot wing those. You write them like you would write a scientific paper — with a clear question, a method, and an honest account of results.

The internal users, the people whose daily work would change when the new system replaced the old one, cared about things nobody had written down. Would the new interface break their habits? Would the handover include documentation they could actually use? Would somebody be available when things broke at 8 AM on a Tuesday? These questions only surfaced when I made a deliberate effort to ask them. They did not come up in steering group meetings.

The technical team, including me, cared about being able to do good work without being interrupted every two hours to explain what we had done in the last two hours. Good project management at this layer looks like absorbing interruptions, translating upward, and keeping the team's surface area toward the rest of the organisation small enough that they can concentrate.

Treating all four groups with the same communication strategy is what makes a project feel chaotic to everyone involved. A project manager is partly a translator. The same underlying event — "we chose Azure Data Factory" — sounds different to each of these audiences, and the project moves faster when each hears it in the form they need.

Governance Is Not Optional, and It Is Not the Enemy

Engineers, as a general rule, find governance tedious. I did too, for a long time. A grant-funded project cured me of this.

When you have external funding, you have audit trails whether you want them or not. The question is not whether the audit trail exists. The question is whether it exists inside your normal working process or as a painful parallel exercise you do at reporting deadlines.

We moved early to put governance inside the working process. Every code change had a ticket. Every architectural decision had a short memo. Every milestone had a completion note with what was delivered, what was deferred, and what was learned. This felt like overhead at the start. It felt like essential infrastructure by month six. When the funder asked for a mid-project review, I did not have to reconstruct anything. I had to assemble something that already existed.

The same principle applies to any IT project with compliance requirements, which is most IT projects in 2026. GDPR, security frameworks, audit requirements: these are not add-ons you do at the end. They are constraints that shape the architecture from day one. A good project manager surfaces these constraints early, makes them visible to the team, and invests in the lightweight tools and documentation patterns that let the team work inside them without noticing.

Governance done well looks like nothing at all. You only see it when it is missing and you need it.

The Tender You Will Not Run, and the One You Will

An Innobooster project is not a public tender, but the structure has more in common with one than you might think. External funding, structured evaluation, external review, formal deliverables, obligations that extend beyond the project itself. I learned several things from managing this structure that I now think apply directly to public-sector tender processes.

First: write requirements for people who are not you. The person reading your requirements document is not in your head. They do not know the context you have been living in for months. Every requirement needs to be testable by someone who arrives fresh. This sounds obvious and is not.

Second: budget for the thing you do not know you need. Every grant project I have seen or heard about had an underestimated line item for integration work. Not the integrations you planned; the integrations you did not know you needed until you started. Reserve 10-15% of your budget for unknowns, not for scope expansion but for the genuine surprises that show up in any real IT project.

Third: evaluate vendors on substance. A technical project manager with hands-on background has an advantage here that is hard to replicate. When a vendor says "our platform natively supports Copilot integration," you can ask the three follow-up questions that distinguish real capability from slideware. When a vendor says "implementation typically takes 6 weeks," you can ask what they are not counting, because you know what 6 weeks of implementation actually contains. This is not about catching vendors out. It is about making procurement decisions that will not surprise you in production.

Handover Is the Project

The last thing I learned, and the one I underestimated most at the start, is that the handover is the project. Everything before it is preparation.

A project that produces a working system but cannot be operated by anyone other than its creators has not delivered value. It has delivered a dependency. The funding body writes a check; the system is built; then the original team moves on and the system gradually degrades until someone quietly rebuilds it from scratch three years later. This pattern is common enough that I now plan for it explicitly.

Good handover has three parts, and each has to start early. The first is runnable documentation: can a new person stand up the system and make it work by following written instructions? If they cannot, your documentation is insufficient, no matter how detailed it looks. Test this by having an unfamiliar colleague try it. The second is operational knowledge: what does this system do when it is healthy, what does it do when it is unhealthy, and what are the recovery procedures for each failure mode? This is the knowledge that lives in the heads of the people who built it. Getting it out of their heads and into a document is a specific discipline, and it has to be scheduled, not left to the end. The third is ownership transfer: who is actually responsible for this system now, and do they know they are? An unclear owner is a system that will not be maintained.

I now think of handover planning as starting in the first week of a project, not the last. The questions "who operates this," "what will they need to operate it," and "how do we get them what they need" should be on the status page from day one. By the time you are writing the final deliverables, the handover should already be half done.

What I Would Tell Someone Starting Their First Grant Project

Three things, in order of importance.

Expect the governance to be a feature, not an overhead. Build it into your working process early. By month three, you will be glad you did.

Write down decisions as you make them. Not later. Not in retrospective. In a dated, stable document that you can search. The person who will thank you for this is the version of you who has to answer a question six months from now.

The technical work and the project work are the same job. When I tried to keep them in separate compartments, the project wobbled. When I accepted that writing a one-page status note and debugging a broken ingestion pipeline were parts of the same responsibility, the work got cleaner and the project got finished.

Innobooster projects exist because someone in government believes that small companies can do ambitious technical work if given the runway. Keeping that premise honest — delivering real transformation within real constraints — is the actual job. Everything else is tooling.
